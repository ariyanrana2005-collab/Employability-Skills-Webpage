Reflection on Employability Skills

The Consumer Journey module and in particular the analysis of the Marks & Spencer's omnichannel approach provided me with a focussed, practical environment to work in to acquire the employability skills required by the module.

The groundwork was the active partaking. When our group mapped out M&S's journey stages, I was able to provide evidenced argument as opposed to opinion because I had attended a series of seminars and workshops on consumer behaviour theory such as Maslow's hierarchy, or the Engel–Blackwell model. Teamwork was a natural extension of that: we did our research, design and delivery in teams, and it was getting one section of the presentation to fit with the argument from the other section about “brand psychology”, while ensuring that no one's ethics were in conflict with each other, was a lesson in how much a group presentation relies on synchronised language and thought (Gibson,Landry and Tuntland, 2024).

Here, where lexical and logical semantics were no longer a desired add-on but became the glue that kept our argument together. The terminology used in the slides was key to keeping our buyer persona separate from the more general discussion of target-audience: I insisted on keeping segmentation and targeting and positioning analytically distinct – and it's this discipline and consistency which held us together when arguing the need for a Sparks loyalty programme rather than the programme's name, rather than its drift between claims. The Q&A process was also logical, as I had to demonstrate, in live session, how a recommendation arose from the evidence, and not just sound convincing.

The ability to manage the workload across the three assessments, including a quiz, a presentation and this reflection, required me to plan backwards from deadlines, rather than reacting to them (Kramer,Hsiao and Stienstra, 2026).This has been followed by career awareness; my investigations into the way M&S use AI personalisation and augmented reality has led me to think that I would like to progress towards a more digital marketing and consumer insight job, instead of the generalist route. Networking was more off-the-cuff, in the form of peer feedback in rehearsals or questions posed by the lecturer, both of which helped me formulate a strategic decision to those not within my own thinking.

Overall, all of these experiences have honed my thinking repertoire, made me more thoughtful about the language, more disciplined with logic, and more focused on my own trajectory namely, a marketing career.

Lexical & logical semantics in practice

Lexical semantics is about precision of word choice and meaning, not conflating "segmentation" with the "targeting". Logical semantics is the consistency of reasoning between a claim and evidence. Sharpened in both senses between quiz and group presentation: slide language became more disciplined, and the justifications for the strategic recommendations came across in a much more easily defensible way when questioned.The importance of collaboration, ethical decision making and continuous learning, and how these skills play a significant role in creating a successful career path in the current marketing landscape (Shifa et al., 2025).

Evidence of development

Five stops on the skills journey

The following sections provide evidence of the employability skills developed through participation in The Consumer Journey module and the Marks & Spencer group presentation.

Awareness Mandatory

Active Participation

Consistent attendance at lectures, seminars and workshops on consumer behaviour theory enabled me to actively participate in the Marks & Spencer group project. By applying concepts such as Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs during our consumer behaviour analysis, I was able to contribute theory-informed ideas during planning discussions and make meaningful contributions to the group's work.

Consideration Optional

Career Awareness & Preparation

Researching Marks & Spencer's use of AI personalisation and augmented reality for the Creative Campaign section increased my understanding of current digital marketing practices. It also helped me identify the knowledge and skills required for future careers in digital marketing and consumer insight, strengthening my confidence and career readiness.

Purchase Mandatory

Managing Workloads

Completing the quiz, group presentation and this reflection within the same assessment period strengthened my workload management skills. By planning my tasks, setting priorities and meeting deadlines, I was able to balance multiple responsibilities while maintaining the quality of my contribution to the Marks & Spencer project.

Retention Mandatory

Teamwork

Working collaboratively on the Marks & Spencer presentation strengthened my teamwork skills. Each group member was responsible for different sections, but we regularly reviewed our work together to ensure our analysis of brand psychology, the consumer journey and the Sparks loyalty programme remained accurate, consistent and well connected throughout the presentation.

Advocacy Optional

Networking

Rehearsing the presentation with classmates and responding to questions from the lecturer helped me build professional relationships and improve my communication skills. Feedback from peers and the lecturer enabled me to refine my explanations, present marketing concepts more confidently and communicate strategic decisions to different audiences.
